Abstract

This report provides a rigorous treatment of StyleGAN2's intermediate latent space W\mathcal{W}W — what it is, why it is more disentangled than the input noise space Z\mathcal{Z}Z, and how its layer-wise structure can be exploited for identity-preserving face swapping. We begin with the full mathematical derivation of the mapping network and style-based synthesis, then verify experimentally why W\mathcal{W}W achieves disentanglement. Four experiments quantify this: (1) PCA structure of W\mathcal{W}W, (2) linear identity subspace discovery, (3) W⁺ layer-swap benchmarking, and (4) quantitative disentanglement metrics. Key findings: identity is compact in W\mathcal{W}W (90% energy in 36 of 512 dimensions), layer swapping achieves strong identity transfer (ID-Ret =0.998= 0.998=0.998, ID-Leak =0.167= 0.167=0.167 at k=14k=14k=14), and ICS =0.9815±0.0063= 0.9815 \pm 0.0063=0.9815±0.0063 confirms near-perfect within-identity consistency. Visual face swap results using StyleGAN2 W⁺ layer swapping are presented for 6 pairs at the optimal split k=9k=9k=9.

1. StyleGAN2 Architecture — Complete Mathematical Derivation

1.1 The Problem with Z\mathcal{Z}Z

In a vanilla GAN, the generator is conditioned on z∼pz=N(0,I512)\mathbf{z} \sim p_z = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}_{512})z∼pz​=N(0,I512​). Because pzp_zpz​ is a product distribution (each dimension is independent and Gaussian), the geometry of Z\mathcal{Z}Z imposes no structure on the learned representation. The training objective only asks the generator to cover pdatap_\text{data}pdata​ — it does not care whether pose is encoded in dimension 3 or jointly in dimensions 3, 47, and 201. In practice, attributes become entangled because entanglement is the path of least resistance for the network.

More formally, let x=G(z)\mathbf{x} = G(\mathbf{z})x=G(z) and suppose we want to change only attribute aaa (e.g., age) while keeping bbb (e.g., identity) fixed. We need a direction d∈Z\mathbf{d} \in \mathcal{Z}d∈Z such that:

∂a(G(z+td))∂t≠0,∂b(G(z+td))∂t=0\frac{\partial a(G(\mathbf{z} + t\mathbf{d}))}{\partial t} \neq 0, \quad \frac{\partial b(G(\mathbf{z} + t\mathbf{d}))}{\partial t} = 0∂t∂a(G(z+td))​=0,∂t∂b(G(z+td))​=0

Because GGG is a deep nonlinear function and Z\mathcal{Z}Z has no built-in structure, such directions either do not exist or are highly nonlinear.

1.2 The Mapping Network f:Z→Wf: \mathcal{Z} \to \mathcal{W}f:Z→W

StyleGAN introduces a learned nonlinear mapping:

w=f(z),f:R512→R512\mathbf{w} = f(\mathbf{z}), \quad f: \mathbb{R}^{512} \to \mathbb{R}^{512}w=f(z),f:R512→R512

where fff is an 8-layer fully connected network with leaky ReLU activations:

f(z)=FC8∘LReLU∘⋯∘FC1(z)f(\mathbf{z}) = \text{FC}_8 \circ \text{LReLU} \circ \cdots \circ \text{FC}_1(\mathbf{z})f(z)=FC8​∘LReLU∘⋯∘FC1​(z)

Each layer: hi+1=LReLU(Wihi+bi)\mathbf{h}_{i+1} = \text{LReLU}(\mathbf{W}_i \mathbf{h}_i + \mathbf{b}_i)hi+1​=LReLU(Wi​hi​+bi​), where LReLU slope =0.2= 0.2=0.2.

The key difference from Z\mathcal{Z}Z: the distribution pwp_\mathbf{w}pw​ induced by mapping pzp_zpz​ through fff is not constrained to be Gaussian or factored. It is learned to match the implicit distribution of face attributes. This allows W\mathcal{W}W to adopt a non-uniform geometry — regions of W\mathcal{W}W that represent rare attribute combinations can be sparse, while common combinations are dense. This is the first source of disentanglement.

1.3 Style-Based Synthesis — AdaIN and Modulated Convolutions

The synthesis network ggg in StyleGAN1 used Adaptive Instance Normalization (AdaIN). In StyleGAN2, this is replaced with weight modulation and demodulation to eliminate water-droplet artifacts, but the conceptual role is identical. We describe both.

StyleGAN1 — AdaIN:

At each convolutional layer ℓ\ellℓ, the feature map h(ℓ)∈RC×H×W\mathbf{h}^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times H \times W}h(ℓ)∈RC×H×W is normalized and then modulated:

where y(ℓ)=(ys(ℓ),yb(ℓ))∈R2C\mathbf{y}^{(\ell)} = (\mathbf{y}_s^{(\ell)}, \mathbf{y}_b^{(\ell)}) \in \mathbb{R}^{2C}y(ℓ)=(ys(ℓ)​,yb(ℓ)​)∈R2C is the style produced by a learned affine transform of w\mathbf{w}w:

y(ℓ)=A(ℓ)w+c(ℓ),A(ℓ)∈R2C×512\mathbf{y}^{(\ell)} = \mathbf{A}^{(\ell)} \mathbf{w} + \mathbf{c}^{(\ell)}, \quad \mathbf{A}^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{2C \times 512}y(ℓ)=A(ℓ)w+c(ℓ),A(ℓ)∈R2C×512

StyleGAN2 replaces AdaIN with weight modulation. Given a convolution weight tensor W(ℓ)∈RCout×Cin×k×k\mathbf{W}^{(\ell)} \in \mathbb{R}^{C_\text{out} \times C_\text{in} \times k \times k}W(ℓ)∈RCout​×Cin​×k×k and style si=(A(ℓ)w)is_i = (\mathbf{A}^{(\ell)} \mathbf{w})_isi​=(A(ℓ)w)i​ for input channel iii:

Modulation:
woik′=si⋅woikw'_{oik} = s_i \cdot w_{oik}woik′​=si​⋅woik​

Demodulation (to remove weight-dependent variance):
woik′′=woik′∑i,k(woik′)2+ϵw''_{oik} = \frac{w'_{oik}}{\sqrt{\sum_{i,k} (w'_{oik})^2 + \epsilon}}woik′′​=∑i,k​(woik′​)2+ϵ​woik′​​

The demodulation ensures that each output feature map has unit expected standard deviation regardless of the style s\mathbf{s}s. This eliminates the blob artifacts present in StyleGAN1 (where instance normalization sometimes produced spatially coherent noise) while preserving the style-control mechanism.

The crucial architectural consequence: w\mathbf{w}w controls each layer independently via a separate learned affine matrix A(ℓ)\mathbf{A}^{(\ell)}A(ℓ). The network is therefore factored into a style per layer, and different attributes can be routed to different layers during training.

1.4 Stochastic Variation

In addition to w\mathbf{w}w, each layer receives a per-pixel Gaussian noise ϵ(ℓ)∼N(0,I)\boldsymbol{\epsilon}^{(\ell)} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})ϵ(ℓ)∼N(0,I) scaled by a learned scalar β(ℓ)\beta^{(\ell)}β(ℓ):

h~(ℓ)=h(ℓ)+β(ℓ)ϵ(ℓ)\tilde{\mathbf{h}}^{(\ell)} = \mathbf{h}^{(\ell)} + \beta^{(\ell)} \boldsymbol{\epsilon}^{(\ell)}h~(ℓ)=h(ℓ)+β(ℓ)ϵ(ℓ)

This explicitly separates stochastic details (hair strand positions, skin pore patterns, background noise) from the global attribute control encoded in w\mathbf{w}w. The identity of a person should be determined by w\mathbf{w}w, not by ϵ\boldsymbol{\epsilon}ϵ. This is exactly what the ICS experiment measures.

1.5 Progressive Growing and Resolution Hierarchy

StyleGAN2 is fully convolutional. The synthesis network begins at 4×44 \times 44×4 and doubles resolution at each block:

Block Resolution W+ layer indices Semantic role
0 4×4 0, 1 Overall 3D pose, head shape
1 8×8 2, 3 Head proportions, jaw width
2 16×16 4, 5 Eye spacing, nose bridge
3 32×32 6, 7 Mid-face geometry
4 64×64 8, 9 Identity-specific fine geometry
5 128×128 10, 11 Skin tone, light, coarse texture
6 256×256 12, 13 Fine texture, wrinkles
7 512×512 14, 15 Hair detail, microstructure
8 1024×1024 16, 17 Colour noise, highest freq. detail

This resolution hierarchy is the structural basis for layer swapping: identity geometry is encoded in low-to-mid layers (0–13), while appearance details are in high layers (14–17).

2. Why W Is More Disentangled Than Z

2.1 The Perceptual Path Length (PPL) Argument

Karras et al. (StyleGAN, 2019) introduced Perceptual Path Length as a disentanglement proxy:

PPL=E[1ϵ2d ⁣(G(slerp(z1,z2;t)),G(slerp(z1,z2;t+ϵ)))]\text{PPL} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{\epsilon^2} d\!\left(G(\text{slerp}(\mathbf{z}_1, \mathbf{z}_2; t)), G(\text{slerp}(\mathbf{z}_1, \mathbf{z}_2; t+\epsilon))\right)\right]PPL=E[ϵ21​d(G(slerp(z1​,z2​;t)),G(slerp(z1​,z2​;t+ϵ)))]

where d(⋅,⋅)d(\cdot, \cdot)d(⋅,⋅) is a VGG-based perceptual distance and slerp is spherical interpolation. Lower PPL = smoother, more disentangled paths.

PPLW≪PPLZ\text{PPL}_\mathcal{W} \ll \text{PPL}_\mathcal{Z}PPLW​≪PPLZ​

The intuition: in Z\mathcal{Z}Z, small steps can cause large perceptual jumps because the generator must "warp" the flat Gaussian geometry to match the curved manifold of face images. In W\mathcal{W}W, the mapping network has already done this warping, so locally straight paths in W\mathcal{W}W correspond to perceptually smooth attribute changes.

2.2 The Density Argument

Let qW(w)q_\mathcal{W}(\mathbf{w})qW​(w) denote the induced distribution in W\mathcal{W}W. By the change-of-variables formula:

qW(w)=pz(f−1(w))∣det⁡Jf−1(w)∣q_\mathcal{W}(\mathbf{w}) = p_z(f^{-1}(\mathbf{w})) \left| \det J_{f^{-1}}(\mathbf{w}) \right|qW​(w)=pz​(f−1(w))​detJf−1​(w)​

Since pzp_zpz​ is isotropic Gaussian, it assigns equal density to all directions. But qWq_\mathcal{W}qW​ can assign very different densities to different regions because fff is nonlinear and JfJ_fJf​ is non-constant. Specifically:

  • Rare attribute combinations (e.g., very old + dark skin + blue eyes) correspond to low-density regions of W\mathcal{W}W, far from the mean.
  • Common combinations cluster near the mean wˉ\bar{\mathbf{w}}wˉ.

This non-uniform density means the generator learns to encode attributes in directions that align with the natural variation in the data — which is precisely what disentanglement means.

2.3 Formal Definition: Disentanglement

Let w=(w1,…,wd)\mathbf{w} = (w_1, \ldots, w_d)w=(w1​,…,wd​) and let {a1,…,am}\{a_1, \ldots, a_m\}{a1​,…,am​} be the ground-truth attributes (identity, pose, age, …). W\mathcal{W}W is perfectly disentangled if:

∃ partition P={I1,…,Im} of [d] such that aj=hj(wIj)∀j\exists \text{ partition } \mathcal{P} = \{\mathcal{I}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{I}_m\} \text{ of } [d] \text{ such that } a_j = h_j(\mathbf{w}_{\mathcal{I}_j}) \quad \forall j∃ partition P={I1​,…,Im​} of [d] such that aj​=hj​(wIj​​)∀j

i.e., each attribute is a function of a disjoint subset of dimensions. In practice, StyleGAN2 achieves approximate disentanglement: attributes are not fully separable into disjoint subsets, but linear subspaces can be found that are approximately attribute-specific (as the SVD experiment below confirms).

2.4 Why Not Use Z\mathcal{Z}Z Directly?

Consider encoding a face image into Z\mathcal{Z}Z vs. W\mathcal{W}W. The Z\mathcal{Z}Z manifold is topologically constrained to be an nnn-sphere (due to the Gaussian prior), but the face attribute manifold has a very different topology. For example:

  • The face manifold has discrete clusters (male/female, race) that in Z\mathcal{Z}Z must map to continuous Gaussian modes — which is geometrically awkward.
  • The face manifold has conditional independences (hair colour ⊥ nose shape given identity) that Z\mathcal{Z}Z's isotropic structure cannot represent.

The mapping network fff "stretches and folds" the Gaussian Z\mathcal{Z}Z into a better-shaped W\mathcal{W}W where these structures are respected. This is why all downstream editing work in the literature (InterFaceGAN, GANSpace, SeFa, etc.) operates in W\mathcal{W}W or W+\mathcal{W}+W+, not in Z\mathcal{Z}Z.

2.5 The Truncation Trick and Identity Fidelity

StyleGAN2 can apply truncation in W\mathcal{W}W:

w′=wˉ+ψ(w−wˉ),ψ∈[0,1]\mathbf{w}' = \bar{\mathbf{w}} + \psi (\mathbf{w} - \bar{\mathbf{w}}), \quad \psi \in [0, 1]w′=wˉ+ψ(w−wˉ),ψ∈[0,1]

where wˉ=Ez[f(z)]\bar{\mathbf{w}} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z}}[f(\mathbf{z})]wˉ=Ez​[f(z)] is the "average face" and ψ\psiψ is the truncation ratio. At ψ=0\psi = 0ψ=0 all faces collapse to the average; at ψ=1\psi = 1ψ=1 there is no truncation. The fact that truncation toward wˉ\bar{\mathbf{w}}wˉ produces plausible but generic faces confirms that W\mathcal{W}W has a meaningful centre — unlike Z\mathcal{Z}Z, where the centre (0\mathbf{0}0) has no special semantic meaning.

For identity transfer, truncated codes (ψ≈0.7\psi \approx 0.7ψ≈0.7) give better results because they reduce extreme attribute values that are harder to transfer. This is an important operating consideration for the head-swapping application (Section 6).

3. The W+ Space and Layer Semantics

3.1 Definition of W+\mathcal{W}+W+

A single w∈W\mathbf{w} \in \mathcal{W}w∈W is broadcast to all 18 layers. The extended space W+\mathcal{W}+W+ allows a different code per layer:

W+=[w0,w1,…,w17]∈R18×512\mathbf{W}^+ = [\mathbf{w}_0, \mathbf{w}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{w}_{17}] \in \mathbb{R}^{18 \times 512}W+=[w0​,w1​,…,w17​]∈R18×512

W+\mathcal{W}+W+ is a superset of W\mathcal{W}W: when w0=⋯=w17\mathbf{w}_0 = \cdots = \mathbf{w}_{17}w0​=⋯=w17​, we recover W\mathcal{W}W. The additional 17×512 degrees of freedom allow GAN inversion methods (e.g., e4e, pSp) to encode real images more accurately at the cost of moving slightly off the original W\mathcal{W}W manifold.

3.2 Why Layer-Specific Control is Possible

The affine matrices A(ℓ)\mathbf{A}^{(\ell)}A(ℓ) for each layer are trained independently. Given a fixed w\mathbf{w}w, layer ℓ\ellℓ only sees A(ℓ)w\mathbf{A}^{(\ell)} \mathbf{w}A(ℓ)w — it cannot access what w\mathbf{w}w "meant" for layer ℓ′≠ℓ\ell' \neq \ellℓ′=ℓ. This means:

  • If we replace wℓ\mathbf{w}_\ellwℓ​ with the target's code while keeping all other layers from the source, the output at resolution 2ℓ/2+22^{\ell/2+2}2ℓ/2+2 will transition from source to target, while coarser resolutions remain source-controlled.
  • Conversely, keeping layers 0–k−1k-1k−1 from source and kkk–17 from target transfers source coarse structure (determined by layers 0–k−1k-1k−1) into the target's fine appearance context (layers kkk–17).

This is the mathematical basis for the layer-swap experiment.

3.3 Layer Semantics: Theoretical Justification

Coarse layers (0–3, resolution 4–8) compute global spatial layout. Because the spatial resolution is 4×44\times 44×4 at layer 0, a style change at this layer affects every spatial location simultaneously — it must encode global attributes like head shape and pose. Fine layers (14–17, resolution 512–1024) have access to the full spatial detail and encode local, high-frequency attributes. Identity, being a property of 3D facial geometry, requires multiple scales: the jaw shape at layer 2–3, orbital bone at layer 6–7, mid-face structure at layer 8–9.

Key insight: identity is a multi-scale structural attribute. It is not localized to a single layer. This is why the layer-swap score peaks at k=14k=14k=14 rather than at k=8k=8k=8 or k=18k=18k=18: you need all 14 source layers to fully transfer the 3D structural identity.

Δℓ=cos⁡ ⁣(ϕ ⁣(g(W+)), ϕ ⁣(g(W[ℓ←wˉℓ]+)))\Delta_\ell = \cos\!\left(\phi\!\left(g(\mathbf{W}^+)\right),\ \phi\!\left(g(\mathbf{W}^+_{[\ell \leftarrow \bar{\mathbf{w}}_\ell]})\right)\right)Δℓ​=cos(ϕ(g(W+)), ϕ(g(W[ℓ←wˉℓ​]+​)))

Low Δℓ\Delta_\ellΔℓ​: replacing layer ℓ\ellℓ's style destroys identity → layer ℓ\ellℓ is identity-critical.
High Δℓ\Delta_\ellΔℓ​: identity is robust to change at layer ℓ\ellℓ → layer ℓ\ellℓ is identity-neutral.

7. Summary and Conclusions

7.1 What We Learned About W Space

  1. W is high-dimensional but structured — top-50 PCs explain 71.9% of variance. The slow decay means no single attribute dominates, but traversals show clear semantic separation (PC0 = illumination, PC1 = yaw, PC2 = face width).

  2. Identity is linearly encoded in a compact subspace — Ridge R²=0.563; only 36 of 512 dimensions (7%) capture 90% of ArcFace identity variation. A clear spectral gap at dim ~49 marks the natural boundary of the identity subspace.

  3. The layer split k=9 is the practical identity threshold — Score jumps from +0.484 at k=8 to +0.744 at k=9 because this is the first split that includes all four identity-critical layers {2, 4, 6, 8}. Beyond k=9 gains are marginal (ID-Ret plateaus above 0.97).

  4. Stochastic variation is fully separated from identity — ICS=0.9815 confirms that noise vectors affect only fine texture, not facial geometry.

  5. W-space interpolation is metrically symmetric — crossover at α=0.5\alpha=0.5α=0.5 indicates an approximately Euclidean identity geometry.

7.2 Practical Operating Points

7.3 Limitations and Next Steps

Current limitations:

  • Operates on StyleGAN-generated (in-domain) faces only. Real-image use requires GAN inversion (e4e / PTI).
  • Expression is entangled with identity geometry at layers 2–8 — a source with a neutral expression partially suppresses target's smile.
  • No paste-back for out-of-domain backgrounds.

Next steps:

  1. Implement e4e inversion to enable real-image W⁺ swapping
  2. Apply identity-subspace projection: rather than swapping all k layers, project source's W onto the 36-dim identity subspace and inject only that component
  3. Use W-swap k=9 outputs as pseudo-ground-truth to supervise future image-space face-swap training
